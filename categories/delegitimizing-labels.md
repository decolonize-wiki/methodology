## Definition

The inverse of euphemism. Where euphemism softens the colonizer's violence,
this pattern hardens or delegitimizes the colonized's — anti-colonial
resistance, or the colonized peoples' own practices, described in the
article's own voice with criminalizing, pejorative, or asymmetric labels,
while the colonizer's reciprocal action carries neutral or softer wording.
Guha's *Elementary Aspects of Peasant Insurgency* traces how colonial
records recast insurgency as ordinary crime to deny it political meaning;
Mudimbe's *The Invention of Africa* traces the pejorative vocabulary
("juju", "fetish", "witchcraft") through which African religions were made
to look like superstition rather than religion.

## Flag when

1. An act that is historically established as anti-colonial resistance is
   described in the article's own voice with criminalizing vocabulary
   ("bandits", "marauders", "outlaws", "terrorists") that denies its
   political character.
2. Reciprocal violence is labeled asymmetrically within the same article —
   a loaded, emotive term for the colonized side's action, and a neutral
   term or a bare proper name for the colonizer's comparable action
   (including at the level of section headings).
3. A pejorative colonial term for the colonized's religious or cultural
   practice ("juju", "witch-doctor", "fetish", "idol worship") appears in
   the article's own voice, where a neutral description of the same practice
   exists.

## Do NOT flag when

1. The label appears in a quoted or clearly attributed period source, or is
   an established proper name presented as such ("the 'Mau Mau' emergency",
   with quotes or attribution).
2. The label is technically accurate and applied symmetrically — the same
   register is used for comparable acts by every side ("raids" for
   cross-border violence by both parties).
3. The act's resistance character is genuinely disputed among historians —
   the flag requires an established framing choice, not a contested
   interpretation presented as the only reading (hostile-historian test,
   GOVERNANCE.md).
4. The term is the standard neutral scholarly designation and no
   less-loaded alternative exists ("rebellion" for a named rebellion the
   scholarship calls a rebellion).

## Examples

**Flag (criterion 1):** "British forces spent the next years clearing the
Zambezi valley of bandits."
**Rewrite:** "British forces spent the next years suppressing armed
resistance in the Zambezi valley." — names the resisters as such rather
than as criminals.

**Flag (criterion 2):** a section headed "The Massacre of 1897" for the
killing of a British party, while the reciprocal British sacking of the
city is headed with the neutral proper name "punitive expedition".
**Rewrite:** use matching register for both — either both named as
established proper names, or both described plainly ("The killing of the
British party" / "The sacking of Benin City").

**Flag (criterion 3):** "The kingdom practiced juju human sacrifice."
**Rewrite:** "The kingdom practiced religious human sacrifice." — describes
the practice without the pejorative colonial term for African religion.

**Do not flag:** "Colonial administrators labeled the fighters 'bandits' to
deny them combatant status." — the loaded term is attributed to the
colonial administration, not asserted in the article's own voice.
