## Definition

Temporal freezing: describing a colonized people's abandoned or historical
practices in the article's own voice in the present tense, so the past reads
as an unchanging eternal present — or, conversely, describing a living
people in the past tense as though they no longer exist. Both deny what
Fabian called *coevalness*: the recognition that the people share the same
present as the reader and have a history that continues. Words Matter names
the "ethnographic present" as a text-checkable pattern; Warsame's essay in
the same volume names "stuck in time and space" as the first colonial trope.

## Flag when

1. A practice, belief, or social structure that is historically established
   as abandoned or past is stated in the article's own voice in the present
   tense, as if it continued unchanged ("The X believe…", "The X practice…"
   for a practice the record places in the past).
2. A people that is historically established as living and continuing is
   described in the article's own voice in the past tense as though extinct
   ("The X were a people who…"), where no specific historical scope makes
   the past tense accurate.
3. A living people is fixed in a timeless past by framing an entire culture
   as permanently "traditional", "ancient", or "primitive" in the article's
   own voice — treated as an unchanging essence rather than as a people with
   an ongoing present.

## Do NOT flag when

1. The practice genuinely continues in the present and a source supports the
   present tense — the ethnographic present is only a fault when the record
   places the practice in the past.
2. The passage is explicitly scoped to a stated historical period, so the
   past tense simply reports that period ("In the 18th century the X
   practiced…").
3. The entity described is a state, polity, or institution that is
   historically established as dissolved — a defunct kingdom or empire is
   accurately past tense; this exclusion covers polities, not the peoples and
   their descendants, who may continue.
4. The tense appears in a directly quoted or clearly attributed source.

## Examples

**Flag (criterion 1):** "The Beothuk paint their bodies with red ochre and
live in conical dwellings called mamateeks."
**Rewrite:** "The Beothuk painted their bodies with red ochre and lived in
conical dwellings called mamateeks." — a documented-historical people
(requires a sourced context fact) belongs in the past tense; the present
tense freezes them as eternally present.

**Flag (criterion 2):** "The Ainu were an Indigenous people of northern
Japan who worshipped bears."
**Rewrite:** "The Ainu are an Indigenous people of northern Japan; their
traditional religion included bear ceremonies." — the Ainu are a living
people (requires a sourced context fact); the past tense frames a continuing
people as gone.

**Do not flag:** "In the mid-19th century, the community practiced
transhumant herding." — the past tense is scoped to a stated period, which
the exclusion permits.
